# AI, the Pentagon, and the Patriot Act: Are We Watching History Repeat Itself?

If you lived through the post-9/11 era, the last few weeks in AI policy might feel uncomfortably familiar. A national security crisis is invoked. Sweeping powers are demanded. Companies and lawmakers are pressured to fall in line — fast. Anyone who pushes back is labeled a risk.

We've seen this before. And we know how it ended.

---

## What Just Happened

In late February 2026, the U.S. Department of Defense demanded that Anthropic — the company behind Claude — accept new contract language allowing its AI to be used for "any lawful purpose." This would have removed two explicit restrictions Anthropic had negotiated into its original $200 million Pentagon contract from July 2025:

1. **No mass domestic surveillance** of American citizens
2. **No fully autonomous weapons systems**

Anthropic refused.

What followed was swift. The Pentagon designated Anthropic a "supply chain risk to national security" — the first time the U.S. government has ever applied that label to an American company. President Trump ordered all government agencies to stop using Anthropic's products. Defense contractors were told to drop Claude immediately.

Within hours, OpenAI announced it had secured its own Pentagon deal. CEO Sam Altman claimed the agreement contained the same two restrictions Anthropic had been fighting for. But the details told a different story — OpenAI's contract used the same "any lawful purpose" language the Pentagon had insisted on, with safeguards relying on existing DoD policies rather than explicit contractual prohibitions.

The backlash was immediate. OpenAI employees signed an open letter supporting Anthropic. Claude shot to #1 on the App Store. A "#QuitGPT" movement drew over 1.5 million users. Altman later admitted the deal was "rushed" and "looked opportunistic and sloppy," and OpenAI amended the contract to add clearer surveillance protections.

As of March 5, 2026, Anthropic is back at the negotiating table with the Pentagon. The outcome is still uncertain.

---

## The Patriot Act: A History Lesson

For anyone under 30, let me paint the picture.

On September 11, 2001, nearly 3,000 people were killed in the worst terrorist attack on American soil. The country was terrified, grieving, and angry. In that environment, Congress passed the USA PATRIOT Act on October 26, 2001 — just 45 days after the attacks.

Many members of Congress later admitted **they didn't even read the bill** before voting on it. The Bush administration made it clear that anyone who voted "no" could be held responsible if another attack happened. The pressure to act — to do *something* — was overwhelming.

The law gave the government extraordinary surveillance powers. Section 215, in particular, allowed the government to collect "any tangible things" — phone records, purchase histories, medical records, library records, financial data — as long as there were "reasonable grounds" to believe they were "relevant to an authorized investigation."

The promise was simple: **This is to keep you safe. Trust us. We won't abuse it.**

---

## What Actually Happened

They abused it.

For over a decade, the NSA used Section 215 to justify the **bulk collection of phone records for virtually every American** — not targeted suspects, not known threats, but everyone. Every call you made, who you called, when, for how long, and from where.

We only learned the full scope because of Edward Snowden. In June 2013, Snowden leaked classified documents revealing:

- The NSA collected phone metadata on **millions of Americans daily**
- The program had been running since at least 2006
- The government's interpretation of "relevant" was so broad that **billions of records** were swept up, even though only a negligible fraction had any connection to terrorism
- The NSA had collected cell site location data **without notifying Congress or the FISA court**

Even the author of the Patriot Act, Representative Jim Sensenbrenner, publicly stated the law was **never intended** to enable mass, suspicionless surveillance.

In 2015, a federal appeals court ruled the NSA's bulk collection program was **unlawful**. Congress passed the USA Freedom Act, which ended the daily bulk collection orders. In 2018, the NSA was forced to **delete millions of records** it had collected without legal authority. By 2020, Section 215 expired entirely due to its sunset clause — but not before nearly two decades of overreach.

There were also **12 confirmed cases** of individual NSA analysts intentionally misusing the surveillance tools — mostly to spy on romantic interests or personal connections. The safeguards that were supposed to prevent abuse didn't.

---

## The Pattern

Here's what the Patriot Act era taught us:

1. **Crisis creates urgency.** After 9/11, the fear was real. But that urgency was weaponized to push through powers that went far beyond what was necessary or intended.

2. **Vague language enables mission creep.** "Relevant to an authorized investigation" sounded reasonable. In practice, it was interpreted to mean "everything, on everyone, all the time."

3. **Promises don't hold.** Officials swore the powers would be used narrowly and responsibly. They weren't. And we didn't find out for over a decade.

4. **Oversight failed.** Congressional oversight committees weren't fully informed. The FISA court operated in secret. The public had no idea what was happening in their name.

5. **Pushback was punished.** Anyone who questioned the program was accused of being soft on terrorism or putting American lives at risk.

---

## Now Look at Today

The parallels are hard to ignore:

- **Urgency is being leveraged.** The framing is national security and global AI competition. The message is: fall in line or you're a threat.

- **Vague language is the battleground.** "Any lawful purpose" sounds benign. But laws change. DoD policies change. What's "lawful" today may not be what's "lawful" tomorrow — and what's "lawful" isn't always what's right.

- **Promises are being made without teeth.** OpenAI's revised contract relies on existing DoD policies for its safeguards. As the Center for Democracy & Technology pointed out, "Nothing in what they've released would prevent those policies from being changed going forward."

- **Oversight is minimal.** The full text of the OpenAI-Pentagon contract hasn't been released publicly. OpenAI's language on autonomous weapons says AI won't be used to direct them "in any case where law, regulation, or Department policy requires human control" — which experts note falls short of an actual ban.

- **Pushback is being punished.** Anthropic refused to remove two specific ethical restrictions. The result? Labeled a national security risk. Blacklisted. Customers pressured to abandon them.

---

## This Isn't a Left or Right Issue

I want to be clear: this isn't about partisan politics.

The Patriot Act was passed with overwhelming bipartisan support. Democrats and Republicans both voted for it. Both parties had members who defended the NSA surveillance programs for years. And both parties had members who eventually fought to reform them.

The question of how much power the government should have over powerful technologies — and how much we should trust "just trust us" as a safeguard — is not a left-right question. It's a power question.

Whether you lean conservative and believe in limited government overreach, or lean progressive and believe in civil liberties protections, the concern is the same: **unchecked power, given in a moment of urgency, tends to expand far beyond its original scope.**

That's not a theory. That's what happened. We lived it.

---

## What I Think We Should Be Asking

I'm not here to tell you what the right answer is. But I think these are the questions worth asking:

- **Should AI companies have explicit, contractual red lines** on how their technology can be used by governments? Or is "any lawful purpose" sufficient?

- **Should the full text of government AI contracts be public?** If these tools affect the rights of citizens, shouldn't citizens know the terms?

- **What happens when "lawful" changes?** Laws and policies shift with administrations. Are we comfortable with safeguards that depend entirely on current policy?

- **Is labeling a company a "national security risk" for refusing to remove ethical restrictions an appropriate use of government power?** Regardless of where you stand politically, that precedent should give everyone pause.

- **What does independent oversight look like for AI in government?** The FISA court was supposed to oversee the Patriot Act programs. It failed. What would meaningful oversight of AI in defense actually require?

---

## The Lesson

The Patriot Act didn't start as a mass surveillance program. It started as a security measure passed in a moment of genuine national fear. The abuses came later — quietly, incrementally, hidden behind classified programs and secret courts.

We're at a similar inflection point with AI. The technology is powerful. The national security applications are real. And the pressure to move fast and ask questions later is immense.

But we've been here before. And last time, it took a whistleblower, a decade of secret overreach, and multiple court rulings to undo the damage.

Maybe this time, we ask the hard questions *before* we hand over the keys.
