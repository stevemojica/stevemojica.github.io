# How Claude Completely Changed My Workflow

I used to start every morning drowning in tabs. Weather app, news feeds, Twitter, Outlook — all open, all fighting for attention before I even got to actual work. That changed when I started automating my entire morning routine with **Claude**.

Now my mornings run on autopilot, and by the time I sit down at my desk, everything I need is already organized and waiting.

---

## 7:00 AM — The Morning Briefing

Every day at 7 AM, Claude kicks off a **morning briefing skill**. No input from me required. It pulls together:

- **Weather forecast** for my area so I know what the day looks like
- **Top news** — headlines I actually care about, filtered from the noise
- **Trending topics in tech** — what the dev and IT community is talking about
- **Trending hashtags** — so I can stay plugged into the conversation on social media

The briefing gets compiled into a clean summary I can scan in under two minutes. Before Claude, this alone used to eat 20–30 minutes of my morning scrolling through multiple apps and feeds.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  ☀️  MORNING BRIEFING — March 4, 2026       │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Weather: 68°F, Partly Cloudy              │
│  Top News: 3 stories                        │
│  Tech Trending: AI Agents, MCP Protocol     │
│  Hashtags: #BuildWithClaude #LocalLLMs      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

## 8:00 AM — Inbox Cleanup via Chrome Extension

At 8 AM, Claude runs a **skill that hooks into my Chrome extension** to access Outlook. This is where the real magic happens.

It scans my entire inbox and **removes all the junk** — newsletters I never read, promotional emails, automated alerts that don't need my attention, and anything that's clearly spam.

What used to be a 200+ email inbox by Monday morning now gets trimmed down to just the messages that matter. I'm not exaggerating when I say this saves me **at least 30 minutes a day** of mindless deleting and scanning subject lines.

The rules aren't hard-coded either. Claude understands context. It knows the difference between a marketing email from a vendor and an actual conversation thread with that same vendor. That nuance is what makes this better than any static email filter I've tried.

---

## 9:00 AM — Urgent Email Triage and Task Creation

This is the one that truly changed the game. At 9 AM, Claude runs another task that goes back through my cleaned inbox and specifically looks for:

- **Urgent emails** — anything flagged, marked high priority, or containing urgent language
- **Time-sensitive messages** — meeting changes, deadlines, approvals needed, escalations

It takes that information, **creates a dedicated page** summarizing each item, and **adds it directly to my task list** for immediate attention.

So by 9 AM, without lifting a finger, I have:

1. A briefing on the day's news and weather
2. A clean inbox with only relevant emails
3. A prioritized task list built from my most critical messages

## Why This Matters

This isn't about being lazy. It's about **protecting focus time**. Every minute I used to spend on inbox management and news scanning was a minute stolen from deep work — writing code, reviewing architecture, mentoring my team.

Claude gave me that time back. And because it runs on a schedule, it's consistent. It doesn't forget, it doesn't skip days, and it doesn't get distracted.

Here's what my morning looked like **before** vs. **after**:

| Time | Before Claude | After Claude |
|------|--------------|--------------|
| 7:00 AM | Scrolling news, weather apps, Twitter | Claude delivers a briefing automatically |
| 7:30 AM | Still scrolling | Already reviewed briefing, moved on |
| 8:00 AM | Opening Outlook, deleting junk emails | Claude cleans inbox via Chrome extension |
| 8:30 AM | Still cleaning inbox | Inbox already clean, skimming what's left |
| 9:00 AM | Finally reading important emails | Claude triages urgent items into my task list |
| 9:15 AM | Trying to figure out priorities | Already working on the highest priority task |

That's roughly **two hours of productive time recovered every single morning**.
