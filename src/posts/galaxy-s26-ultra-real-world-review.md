# Galaxy S26 Ultra: An Average User Take on the Privacy Display Controversy

Let me get this out of the way upfront: **I am not a tech influencer.** I don't have a ring light. I don't unbox phones on camera. I'm just an average person who bought the Galaxy S26 Ultra on launch day, March 11, 2026, and has been using it every day since.

If you've been anywhere near tech Twitter or YouTube lately, you've probably seen the storm around the S26 Ultra's display. Words like "downgrade," "worse," and "deal-breaker" get thrown around constantly. I wanted to share what the experience actually looks like from someone who just uses their phone. No microscope comparisons, no lab testing, just real life.

---

## The Privacy Display: What It Actually Is

The Galaxy S26 Ultra is the first smartphone with a built-in Privacy Display. The way it works is hardware-based. The screen's pixels are split into two groups: **Narrow Pixels** that emit light straight forward and **Wide Pixels** that emit light at wider angles. When you turn Privacy Display on, the wide-angle pixels dim significantly, making it nearly impossible for someone sitting next to you on the train or at a coffee shop to see what's on your screen.

It's a genuinely useful feature. But it comes with trade-offs, and that's where the controversy started.

---

## The Brightness Situation

Here's what the critics are right about: **when Privacy Display is on, maximum brightness drops.** That's just physics. If you're dimming half the pixels to block side-angle viewing, you're going to lose brightness. Samsung rates the screen at up to 2,600 nits, but with the privacy filter active, that number gets cut roughly in half.

I live in Southern California. We get a lot of sun. And yes, I did notice that when I had Privacy Display running full-time outdoors in direct sunlight, the screen wasn't as bright as I'd expect from a flagship phone.

**But here's the thing. I don't need privacy mode on 24/7.**

---

## How I Actually Use Privacy Display

Instead of leaving it on all the time, I set it to **app-based mode**. This means Privacy Display only activates for specific things:

- **Password managers** for when I'm pulling up credentials
- **Banking apps** for when I'm checking balances or making transfers
- **Notifications** so the person next to me can't read my messages

The rest of the time? It's off. Full brightness, full viewing angles, no compromises. This is how the feature was designed to be used, and once I set it up this way, the "brightness problem" completely disappeared from my daily experience.

If you just got an S26 Ultra and you're frustrated by the display looking dim, check your Privacy Display settings. Going app-based instead of always-on makes a huge difference.

---

## The Resolution Thing Nobody Tells You

Here's another thing that caught me off guard when I first set up the phone: **the Galaxy S26 Ultra ships set to Full HD+ (FHD+) resolution by default.**

This phone has a gorgeous 6.9-inch QHD+ AMOLED display capable of 3120 x 1440 resolution. But out of the box, Samsung has it dialed down to FHD+ to save battery. A lot of people complaining about text sharpness or the screen looking "off" compared to their old phone might not have switched it over yet.

**Go to Settings > Display > Screen Resolution and switch it to QHD+.** You'll immediately notice crisper text and sharper details. And unlike older Samsung phones, you still get the full 120Hz refresh rate at QHD+. No compromises.

This one simple change made a noticeable difference for me. If you're coming from an S25 Ultra that was already set to QHD+, and your new S26 Ultra is at FHD+ by default, of course the screen is going to look worse.

---

## Is the Display Actually Worse?

Honestly? No. Not in any way that matters for daily use.

Are there measurable differences in the pixel layout because of the privacy hardware? Yes, technically. Some reviewers with microscopes have shown that the subpixel arrangement is different from the S25 Ultra. Samsung themselves acknowledged there may be "some variation" at extreme brightness and certain angles.

But in the two weeks I've been using this phone, reading articles, watching YouTube, scrolling social media, taking and viewing photos, **I have not once thought the display looked bad.** It's bright, it's smooth, the colors are great. Is it the single best display ever put on a phone? Maybe not by a razor-thin lab measurement. Is it an excellent display that I enjoy using every day? Absolutely.

---

## The AI Features: Cool but Still Early

The S26 Ultra is being marketed as the first "agentic AI phone," and it comes loaded with Google's Gemini screen automation. The headline feature is that you can tell Gemini to do multi-step tasks. It'll open apps, navigate menus, and complete actions for you in a secure background window.

I tested the **Starbucks ordering** demo that's been making the rounds. I told Gemini to order my usual drink, and it:

1. Opened the Starbucks app
2. Found my nearest store
3. Navigated to the right drink
4. Added it to the cart
5. Waited for me to hit confirm

And it worked. Pretty smoothly, actually. Gemini even asked me to clarify the size when I didn't specify.

**But here's my honest take:** it's faster to just do it yourself. I can open the Starbucks app, tap my favorite order, and confirm in about 15 seconds. Watching Gemini navigate through the same steps took over a minute. It's impressive as a tech demo. It's cool that it works. But at this point, it's a gimmick more than a time-saver.

I haven't really dug into the other AI features yet. I'm sure there are use cases where Gemini automation genuinely saves time, maybe complex multi-app tasks or things you do rarely and don't have muscle memory for. But for everyday stuff like ordering coffee? I'll keep doing it the old-fashioned way.

---

## The Bottom Line

The Galaxy S26 Ultra is a great phone. The Privacy Display controversy is overblown. It's a brand-new hardware feature with real trade-offs, but once you configure it properly (app-based, not always-on) and make sure your resolution is set to QHD+, the experience is excellent.

I'm not going to tell you it's the greatest phone ever made. I'm not going to tell you the display is flawless. What I will tell you is that as an average person who just wants a phone that works well, looks good, and lasts all day, **I'm enjoying it, and I have zero regrets.**

If you're on the fence, stop watching the outrage videos and go try one in person. Your eyes are going to be a better judge than any YouTube thumbnail.
